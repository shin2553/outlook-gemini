Introduction
This operating manual describes the SCANLAB 
camera adapter 
• # 127502
(objective unit, beam splitter housing incl. beam 
splitter and two adapter plates).
This manual is a part of the product. Please read these 
instructions carefully before you proceed with 
installing and operating the camera adapter. In 
particular observe all safety guidelines in this manual. 
If there are any questions regarding the contents of 
this manual, please contact SCANLAB (see page 19).
Keep the manual available for servicing, repairs and 
product disposal. This manual should accompany the 
product if ownership changes hands. 
SCANLAB reserves the right to update this operating 
manual at any time and without notification.
1.1
Product Overview
SCANLAB camera adapters are designed for camera-
based observation of the working field of a scan 
head.
The camera adapter in the above listed product confi-
guration can be attached to the beam entrance of a 
SCANcube or an excelliSCAN-scan head from 
SCANLAB.  
The objective unit includes a camera objective with a 
focal length of f = 105 mm (for a wavelength of 
450 nm - 950 nm), an adjustable iris diaphragm, 
provisions for installing a C-mount-compatible 
camera (or if necessary for installing C-mount 
adapters) and provisions for installing color filters.
The technical specifications of the product are 
summarized on page 20.

repackaged and returned to SCANLAB.

Principle of Operation
2.1
Intended Usage
The camera adapter can be attached to the beam 
entrance of a SCANLAB scan head for camera-based 
observation of the working field (if the scan head is 
equipped with a scan objective). For observation, the 
camera adapter optic decouples the light arriving 
from the illuminated working field and subsequently 
passing through the scan head’s scan objective and 
scan mirrors to the beam entrance. The portion of the 
working field capturable by the camera depends on 
the scan mirrors’ positions.
2.2
Mechanical Design
The camera adapter essentially consists of four 
components: 2 adapter plates, a beam-splitter 
housing and an objective unit (also see figure 1).
The two adapter plates serve as interfaces for 
mounting the camera adapter between scan head 
and laser flange.
The holes of the adapter plate for attaching the scan 
head to the camera adapter’s beam-exit side are 
compatible with mounting holes of the SCANcube or 
excelliSCAN scan heads from SCANLAB.
To facilitate observation, the beam splitter housing 
contains a dichroic beam splitter that couples light 
exiting the illuminated working field. The laser wave-
length is allowed to travel unhindered, but illumina-
tion wavelengths reflected from the working plane 
are decoupled to the camera.
The objective unit attached to the beam splitter 
housing’s upper side contains a camera objective, an 
adjustable iris diaphragm and a translation stage. A 
camera can be attached to the flange with C-mount 
thread on the objective unit’s upper side. The objec-
tive unit’s underside contains a threaded notch for 
attaching a color filter.
The light decoupled by the beam splitter is directed 
through the camera objective and onto the active 
imaging surface of the camera (e.g. CCD chip). 
Camera image focusing is achieved manually by 
rotating the translation stage’s focus ring. The adjust-
able iris diaphragm and provision for a color filter are 
intended for optimizing image quality. Performed 
translation-stage and iris-diaphragm adjustments 
can be fixed via the corresponding group of three set 
screws. 
Additionally, the orientation of the camera (position-
able around the objective’s optical axis) can be fixed 
via three set screws.
The orientation of the beam splitter and its housing 
(relative to the two adapter plates) can also be 
adjusted so that the housing’s opening points down 
or sideways instead of up.

2.3
Observation Field Size
The size dv of the observation field, i.e. the size of the 
portion of the working field visible to the camera, can 
be calculated via the camera chip’s size dc, the scan 
objective’s focal length fso and the camera objectives 
focal length fco at the used observation wavelength, 
in accordance with the following formula: 
dv = dc · fso / fco
For example, a camera objective with fco = 105 mm, 
a scan objective with fso = 160 mm and a 1/2’’ chip 
(6.4 mm x 4.8 mm(1)) produce a 9.7 mm x 7.3 mm 
observation field.
Diverse apertures in the system (e.g. beam entrance, 
mirror, iris diaphragm, objective apertures) can 
substantially reduce observation field edge bright-
ness, particularly in conjunction with large imaging 
chips and camera objectives of short focal length.
2.4
Observation Field Position
The position of the observation field is adjusted by 
moving the scan head’s deflection mirrors. Ideally, 
the observation field is centered with respect to the 
laser's point of arrival. However, mechanical toler-
ances and laser beam adjustment errors can result in 
an offset (that is constant across the entire observa-
tion field). Moreover, the scan objective's chromatic 
aberration can produce an additional offset which 
increases with increasing difference between the 
laser's wavelength and the illumination wavelength. 
This additional offset is null at the working field's 
centerpoint and increases radially toward the edge of 
the working field.
2.5
Imaging Quality
Working-field illumination light reaching the scan 
head’s scan objective will exit more or less parallel, 
and (with the exception of laser-wavelength light) be 
deflected by the dichroic beam splitter to the camera 
objective. The decoupling efficiency is somewhat 
reduced for the wavelength range used by pointer 
lasers: although the beam splitter decouples a large 
part of the laser power and passes it to the camera, 
a small part is transmitted. Therefore, the pointer is 
visible in the working field, although attenuated.
After reflection at the beam splitter, the light is 
directed through the camera objective onto the 
camera’s active imaging surface (e.g. a CCD chip). 
Camera image focusing is achieved manually by 
rotating the focus ring to adjust the distance 
between the camera objective and the camera. 
Imaging quality can be further optimized by 
adjusting the iris diaphragm’s opening.
Camera image quality is also heavily influenced by the 
scan objective’s properties and working field illumi-
nation. Unlike camera objectives, scan objectives are 
generally not color-corrected and produce relatively 
large color errors. Therefore, illumination should be 
restricted to as narrow a wavelength range as 
possible (e.g. LEDs at 880 nm); otherwise an indis-
tinct image will be produced. The light’s bandwidth 
should be further narrowed by placing a color or 
interference filter between the beam splitter and the 
camera, though this attenuates camera image bright-
ness. Additionally, the illumination wavelength 
should not differ too widely from the laser wave-
length. On selecting the scan system, also take care, 
that the scan mirrors’ reflectivity for the illumination 
wavelength is high enough. With dielectric mirrors 
the pointer laser range (630 nm - 670 nm) often can 
be used for illumination. Please refer to the specifica-
tions of your scan system’s scan mirrors.
Resolution is affected not only by the optics, but also 
the camera chip pixel size.
(1)
For historical reasons, the chip name (in inch) does not agree 
with the real chip size. Typical chip sizes are:
1/3’’ chip: 4.8 mm x 3.6 mm
1/2’’ chip: 6.4 mm x 4.8 mm
2/3’’ chip: 8.8 mm x 6.6 mm

Checking the Specifications
Make sure the specifications of the camera adapter 
meet the requirements of your application (see “Tech-

• Protect the camera adapter from dust and other

3.3
Mounting the Camera 
Adapter
The illustrations for this section are found in the 
previous section, starting on page 8. Figure 2 shows 
the camera adapter with its mounting assembly and 
dimensions. The laser system flange, alignment pins 
and mounting screws are not included in the 
package.
The two adapter plates facilitate attachment of the 
camera adapter to the scan head and mounting of 
the camera adapter together with the scan head onto 
the laser flange.
The adapter plate for attaching the scan head to the 
camera adapter’s beam-exit side is equipped with 
bore holes compatible with the mounting holes of 
the SCANcube or excelliSCAN scan heads from 
SCANLAB.
Note: SCANLAB also offers a beam-exit side adapter plate 
equipped with bore holes compatible with the mounting 
holes of the hurrySCAN, hurrySCAN II, intelliSCAN or 
SCAN gine scan heads.
The two adapter plates and the beam entrance and 
exit are oriented relative to another so as to compen-
sate the beam displacement produced by the beam 
splitter (see figure 3 on page 9). That means a laser 
beam that enters centered (perpendicular to the 
beam entrance plane) will also exit centered at the 
beam exit side. Of course, the beam displacement is 
also compensated for camera monitoring.
1. Mounting to the Scan Head
The camera adapter should first be mounted onto the 
scan head. Therefore, first attach the camera 
adapter’s beam-exit side adapter plate to the scan 
head, and afterward the beam splitter housing to this 
adapter plate.
To facilitate mounting at the scan head, the beam-
exit side adapter plate contains two 4H7 alignment 
holes and four 5.5 mm mounting holes. To facilitate 
mounting the beam splitter housing, the adapter 
plate contains four 4H7 alignment holes and four 
6.6 mm mounting holes. Mount the camera adapter 
in the following manner:
Carefully remove the protective cap at the beam 
exit side (e.g. with a small screwdriver).
If the beam-exit side adapter plate is mounted on 
the beam splitter housing, then dismount it. The 
adapter plate is attached to the beam splitter 
housing via four M6 screws and two 4h6 
alignment pins.
Place two 4h6 alignment pins in the corre-
sponding 4H7 alignment holes on the scan head 
or on the adapter plate.
Place the adapter plate on the scan head so that 
the alignment pins line up with the alignment 
holes on the scan head and the adapter plate. The 
adapter plate’s beam exit must be centered over 
the scan head’s beam entrance (see figure 4).
Fasten the adapter plate to the scan head by 
installing two mounting screws with M5 threads 
into the corresponding threaded mounting holes 
of the scan head.

Carefully remove the protective cap at the camera

Place two 6h6 alignment pins in the corre-

the adapter plate.
Place the camera adapter together with the scan 
head on the flange so that the alignment pins line 
up with the alignment holes on the flange and 
the adapter plate. This way the camera adapter is 
aligned with respect to the laser.
Fasten the camera adapter to the flange by 
installing two mounting screws with M6 threads 
into the corresponding threaded mounting holes 
of the adapter plate.
3.4
Adjusting the Objective Unit’s 
Orientation
Typically, the beam splitter housing’s opening and the 
objective unit of the delivered camera adapter point 
upward. If cable-layout conflicts or other reasons 
necessitate another direction, then the beam splitter 
and its housing can be adjusted to point sideways or 
down instead of up.
Thereby, independent of the objective unit’s selected 
orientation, the relative orientation of the two 
adapter plates (i.e. the relative orientation of beam 
entrance and exit) remains so that the beam displace-
ment produced by the beam splitter will be compen-
sated. This even applies if the two adapter plates are 
additionally rotated relative to another.
Caution!
• To align and fasten the camera adapter, only use 
the alignment and mounting holes depicted in 
figure 3 on page 9. Other holes cannot be used 
for aligning and fastening the camera adapter 
or for any other purposes.
Caution!
• Perform the described adjustment only in a 
clean, dust-free environment.
• Never touch the beam splitter’s optical surface. 
When disassembling/assembling, exercise parti-
cular care not to damage the beam splitter.
• Dirt on the beam splitter can increase the 
absorption of laser power by its optical surfaces. 
Burned-in dirt can lead to destruction of the 
beam splitter and further consequences.

3.5
Mounting a Camera
A camera is not included in the delivered package. It 
can be selected according to customer requirements.
The camera should be sensitive to the illumination 
wavelength. Be especially sure the camera doesn't 
contain an internal filter that unintentionally attenu-
ates light at the illumination wavelength. Some 
cameras, for instance, are equipped with internal 
filters that restrict the usable wavelength range to 
that of visible light. Here, observation at wavelengths 
such as 880 nm would be nearly impossible.
To facilitate mounting a camera, the objective unit’s 
upper side contains a flange with a C-mount adapter. 
For adjusting the distance between the camera and 
camera objective, one or more C-mount extensions 
are generally included in the delivered package. The 
camera and optional C-mount extensions can be 
simply threaded onto the objective unit’s top side. 
Supplied C-mount extensions are already mounted.
Adjustment of the camera is described in chapter 4.4, 
page 17.
3.6
Providing Illumination
Illumination of the working field is not part of the 
delivered package and can be chosen in accordance 
with customer requirements.
Illumination should be restricted to as narrow a 
wavelength range as possible (e.g. LEDs at 880 nm); 
otherwise the produced image could be indistinct, 
particularly at the edges (see chapter 2.5, page 6). 
The light’s bandwidth should be further narrowed by 
placing a color or interference filter between the 
beam splitter and the camera, though this attenuates 
camera image brightness. Additionally, the illumi-
nation wavelength should not differ too widely from 
the laser wavelength. On selecting the scan system, 
also take care, that the scan mirrors’ reflectivity for 
the illumination wavelength is high enough.
The lighting should be arranged to illuminate the 
observed field as homogenously as possible.
If there are any questions regarding the illumination, 
please contact SCANLAB (see page 19).

3.7
Mounting a Color Filter
Color filters are not included in the delivered package 
(this also applies to color filter retention rings and 
color filter mounts). 
Color filter retention rings, e.g. for 1-inch and 2-inch 
color filters, as well as color filter mounts are option-
ally available from SCANLAB, find the relevant article 
numbers in the text below. 
Color filters are supplied by the customer suiting the 
illumination and meeting the requirements. Color 
filters should be selected that have as narrow a trans-
mission band as possible. The aperture of the color 
filter should be large enough to avoid vignetting.
Mounting a color filter requires unscrewing the 
complete objective unit from the beam-splitter 
housing. At the bottom of the objective unit the 
holding ring is screwed in. 
For example, a 2-inch color filter (max. outer diameter 
50.8 mm; thickness up to 4 mm) is inserted into its 
notch (height 6.5 mm). For fixation, a fitting color 
filter retention ring (SCANLAB article number 
100092) has to be screwed into the M52 thread (see 
figure 6). 
No part of color filter, color filter retaining ring and if 
applicable, color filter mount must protude from the 
range indicated by 13.5 in figure 6. Otherwise the 
optical elements are damaged.
1-inch color filters (max. outer diameter 25.4 mm; 
thickness 5.5 mm to 8 mm) are mounted by placing 
them into a color filter mount (SCANLAB article 
number 112910) first and then fixing it by a color 
filter retention ring (SCANLAB article number 
107283) . Subsequently, this assembly is screwed into 
the M52 thread located at the bottom end of the 
objective unit. Make sure not to damage the optical 
elements.

• vignetting of the laser beam

4.4
Optimizing Image Quality
Camera Orientation
The image is rotated relative to the working field as a 
consequence of beam deflection at the scan mirrors 
and beam splitter. To compensate this rotation, at 
least in the scan mirrors’ neutral position, the 
mounted camera can be rotated around the camera 
objective’s axis. The desired camera orientation can 
then be fixed via three set screws (see chapter 2.2, 
page 5).
Distance between Camera and Camera Objective
Camera image focusing is achieved manually by 
rotating the focus ring to adjust the distance 
between the camera objective and the camera (via 
the translation stage). The distance can be thereby 
varied about ±6 mm. Performed focusing adjust-
ments can be fixed via three set screws  (see 
chapter 2.2, page 5).
Unlike camera objectives, scan objectives are 
generally not color-corrected and produce relatively 
large color errors. Therefore, the scan objective’s 
focal length and the subsequent distance between 
the camera and camera objective are strongly influ-
enced by the illumination wavelength. Generally, the 
scan objective’s focal length increases with higher 
wavelengths. Longer wavelengths therefore gene-
rally require a larger distance between the camera 
objective and camera. When required, the distance 
can also be varied via various C-mount extensions 
(see chapter 3.5, page 13). Note, that supplied
C-mount extensions are already mounted and may 
need to be removed.
As a starting point for distance adjustment, the 
C-mount extensions included in the delivered 
package should be combined such that the sum of 
the extensions is as close as possible to the recom-
mended C-mount extension value (see technical 
specifications on page 20). If rotation of the focus 
ring doesn’t result in a sharp image, then it might be 
necessary to use a different combination of the 
included C-mount extensions or possibly other 
C-mount extensions. If required, the following 
C-mount extensions are available from SCANLAB: 
5 mm, 11 mm and 22.1 mm.
Iris Diaphragm
Further optimization of image quality can be 
achieved by adjusting the built-in iris diaphragm’s 
opening. Performed iris diaphragm adjustments can 
be fixed by three set screws (see chapter 2.2, page 5).
Note that a narrower iris-diaphragm opening results 
in a sharper but darker image with vignetting at the 
edges.
Illumination
The illumination, too, strongly influences image 
quality (see chapter 2.5, page 6 and chapter 3.6, 
page 13).

Routine Maintenance and Customer Service
5.1
Routine Maintenance of the 
Optical Surfaces
A dirty camera objective or beam splitter diminishes 
camera image quality. 
In addition, dirt on the beam splitter can increase the 
absorption of laser power by its optical surfaces. 
Burned-in dirt can lead to destruction of the beam 
splitter and further consequences. The warranty does 
not cover any damage due to improper use, cleaning 
or handling.
Routine Maintenance of the Beam 
Splitter
The beam splitter is an especially sensitive compo-
nent and should not be touched or removed from the 
beam splitter block. Nevertheless, you must regularly 
inspect the beam splitter for dirt, dust and other 
contaminants.
For this purpose, the objective unit must be 
unscrewed from the beam-splitter housing and, 
under some circumstances, the camera adapter must 
be detached from the laser flange.
If inspection reveals dust particles on the beam 
splitter’s surface, then remove them via an air jet with 
clean, oil-free air.
If the dust cannot be removed in this manner, or if 
inspection reveals more serious contamination, then 
contact SCANLAB for guidance. In extreme cases, the 
complete camera adapter must be returned to 
SCANLAB for inspection and cleaning of the beam 
splitter. However, in some situations SCANLAB might 
be able to recommend a user-performed special 
cleaning procedure.
Routine Maintenance of the Camera 
Objective
The outermost optical surfaces of the camera objec-
tive must be regularly inspected for dirt, dust and 
other contaminants.
For this purpose, the objective unit must be 
unscrewed from the beam-splitter housing and the 
iris diaphragm opened.
If inspection reveals dust particles on the beam 
splitter’s surface, then remove them via an air jet with 
clean, oil-free air.
If the dust cannot be removed in this manner, or if 
inspection reveals more serious contamination, then 
contact SCANLAB for guidance. In extreme cases, the 
complete camera adapter must be returned to 
SCANLAB for inspection and cleaning of the camera 
objective. However, in some situations SCANLAB 
might be able to recommend a user-performed 
special cleaning procedure.
Caution!
• Never touch the optical surfaces of the beam 
splitter and camera objective.
• Conduct checking and perform cleaning only in 
a clean, dirt-free environment.
