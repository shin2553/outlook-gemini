1.6
Product Overview
1.6.1Application
The varioSCAN II is designed for use in a laser scan 
system. It enables the laser beam to be focused and 
the laser spot to be moved quickly and precisely 
along the optical axis. It thereby supplements an 
XY scan system (scan head or scan module) to form a 
3-axis scan system.
The varioSCAN II’s optical parameters are optimized 
for a specific scan system configuration and designed 
either for use with an objective or for use without an 
objective(1). Depending on the optical configuration 
of the complete laser scan system, the varioSCAN II 
serves to maintain the laser focus in a planar target 
area and/or to shift the laser focus within the working 
volume in the Z-direction for 3D applications.
1.6.2Components
The varioSCAN II consists of a dynamic focusing unit 
(motorblock with diverging optic, optional water-
cooled entrance aperture and objective) and a suit-
able servo control board. These two components 
together form a functional unit.
The following two variants are available:
(1) The varioSCAN II supplied with a separate servo 
control board.
(2) The varioSCAN II supplied with an electronic box 
containing the servo control board and a digital 
interface board. The box protects the electronic 
boards from moisture, dust and corrosive vapors, 
as well as damage due to mechanical stress.

2.1.1Conformity to EC Guidelines for 
Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC)
The varioSCAN II is supplied as an OEM component 
intended for installation in a machine (usually a laser 
system). The varioSCAN II must not be put into oper-
ation until it has been installed in a machine that 
complies with the provisions of all applicable direc-
tives and standards.
Neither the varioSCAN II nor its servo control board 
(nor an optionally supplied interface board) are 
shielded against electromagnetic fields. This also 
applies if the cards are installed in SCANLAB's elec-
tronic box.  
After installing the varioSCAN II, the manufacturer of 
the entire system must ensure that electromagnetic 
compatibility is maintained, for example by:
• attaching shielding or plates
• installing the varioSCAN II (incl. servo control 
board and optional interface board or optional ) 
in an EMC-compatible housing
• servo control using EMC-compliant cabling to 
electrically connect the varioSCAN II
• assessing the overall system to establish confor-
mity with relevant technical requirements and 
applicable EMC directives.
2.1.2Complying with Relevant Stan-
dards for the CE Label
The varioSCAN II is delivered as an OEM component 
conceived of for integration into a laser scan system.
The system manufacturer bears the responsibility for 
complying with the standards and guidelines 
required for equipment usage and for the CE label.
Note:
If the varioSCAN II is used without structural 
measures for shielding against electromagnetic 
fields, then SCANLAB views this as an impermis-

2.2
Electrical Safety
Power is furnished to the varioSCAN II by a low 
voltage power supply. The power supply unit must 
meet the following mains insulation requirements:
• If the connectors are covered and cannot be 
reached without tools from the outside, single 
insulation between the mains and the low voltage 
circuit is sufficient. The mains insulation must be 
able to withstand a test voltage of 2 kV AC 
applied between the mains and the low voltage 
circuit.
• If the connectors for low voltage signals can be 
reached from the outside, then double or rein-
forced insulation between the mains and the low 
voltage circuit is necessary. The mains insulation 
must be able to withstand a test voltage of 4 kV 
AC applied between the mains and the low 
voltage circuit.
Additional application-dependent guidelines and 
standards may apply.

Principle of Operation
3.1
Mechanical and Optical 
Overview
The varioSCAN II is an optical system with a dynami-
cally variable focal length. It consists of the following 
major parts (see figure 1):
(1) motor block with a movable diverging optic and 
clamping surface for mounting the varioSCAN II
(2) objective consisting of a focus ring and a static 
focusing optic
(3) optional water-cooled entrance aperture (water-
cooled aperture)
3.1.4Operating Principle
The operating principle is shown in figure 2 on 
page 15. The entering laser beam is expanded by the 
movable diverging optic (a negative focal length 
lens). Next, the beam is collimated and focused by the 
objective (consisting of a lens system with positive 
focal length).
The varioSCAN II’s (total) focal length, and thus also 
the emerging beam’s divergence and convergence 
angles, can be varied by moving the diverging optic. 
This is achieved with a motor regulated by the PC 
controller.
SCANLAB provides a travel range in the beam direc-
tion that suits the customer’s particular application 
by selecting optics with appropriate characteristics.
The user can fine-tune the working distance by manu-
ally turning the varioSCAN II’s focus ring, which 
moves the objective lens along the optical axis of the 
varioSCAN II (see "Adjusting the Working Distance" 
on page 63).
For matching the varioSCAN II’s output beam diam-
eter to the entrance aperture of an XY system (scan 
head or scan module), SCANLAB chooses an optical 
configuration with a suitable expansion factor (see 
Part 2 – Technical Specifications). Additionally, it may 
be necessary to place a beam expander into the beam 
path for this purpose (see figure 30 on page 60).
A water-cooled entrance aperture (water-cooled 
aperture) may be required to dissipate the heat 
absorbed from laser energy (see chapter 4.2 on 
page 25).
When mounting the varioSCAN II (see chapter 4.1 on 
page 22), only use the clamping surface indicated in 
figure 1.

3.2.2Internal Protective Functions
Assuring Reliable Power
For safe operation of the varioSCAN II, a reliable 
supply of power is absolutely essential. If the supply 
voltages deviate excessively from their specified 
values (30 V or 48V) or if the mechanism for 
switching on power does not result in symmetrical 
turn-on of the supply voltages, then unintended 
movements of the diverging optic may occur. This in 
turn can damage the varioSCAN II or the optics of the 
scan system and can (if the laser is on) lead to an 
unintended spot position.
The varioSCAN II’s supply voltages are monitored by 
the digital servo electronics. If, during operation, the 
supply voltage falls below a minimum of approx. 
25 V (e.g. due to excessively long or thin cables, a 
weak power supply or high loads), then the elec-
tronics disconnects the varioSCAN II from the power 
supply. To restart the varioSCAN II, the supply voltage 
problem must first be resolved and the varioSCAN II 
switched off and then restarted.
Monitoring Position Range and Proper 
Operation
The varioSCAN II has a built-in monitoring function to 
prevent damage to its motor or electronics when a 
problem occurs.
If the motor’s position exceeds the allowed position 
range due to an operational disturbance or if the 
diverging optic is not moved to its set position within 
a certain time, then the output stage of the servo 
electronics will be shut down. In such situations, the 
varioSCAN II can no longer be controlled.
Danger!
If the output stage is shut down due to position 
range overrun or any other problem, laser 
power must be switched off immediately. 
Otherwise, serious health hazards and severe 
equipment damage can occur due to uncon-
trolled laser radiation.

3.3.1Integration into a 3-Axis Scan

The varioSCAN II is designed to supplement an XY 
scan system (scan head or scan module) to form a 
3-axis scan configuration.
The XY scan system deflects the laser beam in the XY 
directions and the varioSCAN II or an optional scan 
lens focuses the beam at the desired location. During 
the XY scan system’s scanning process, the 
varioSCAN II’s diverging optic can move highly 
dynamically along the optical axis. Together with the 
stationary focusing optic, this produces a change to 
the system’s overall focal length, synchronized with 
the XY system’s mirrors.
varioSCAN II PR
The varioSCAN II PR can replace a flat field objective 
(F-Theta objective) and enable processes in a plane 
perpendicular to the optical axis of the XY scan 
system. Here, the varioSCAN II PR is used to focus the 
laser beam and maintain the laser focus in a planar 
target area (see figure 3).
The varioSCAN II PR can additionally be used to shift 
the laser spot in the Z direction within a certain 
working volume (i.e. to shift the working plane). It 
thereby transforms an XY scan system into a 3D beam 
deflection system and enables processing in three 
dimensions (see figure 4).

facturer-specified for conductance < 20 µS/cm and 
water hardness < 5 mg/l CaCO3 to offer effective 
protection against corrosion for stainless steel, 
copper, non-ferrous metals and aluminum as well as 
protection against bacteria, algae and fungi. The 
NALCO Yelmet Kit’s organic corrosion inhibitor 
promotes rapid formation of an anti-corrosive film.
The coolant’s quality needs to be checked at regular 
intervals. It might be necessary to periodically replace 
the coolant. Establish suitable maintenance intervals.
For advice on appropriate coolants, refer to the infor-
mation in "Cooling Water Specifications (optional)" 
on page 71) when contacting manufacturers of 
coolant additives (e.g. NALCO, Grünbeck or DOW 
Chemical Company: www.nalco.com, www.gruen-
beck.de, www.dow.com).
Caution!
• An unsuitable coolant can produce blockage or 
corrosion of the cooling channels.
• If, contrary to the above recommendations, 
ordinary tap water is used as coolant, then 
formation of lime and scale deposits can be 
expected (depending on the water’s hardness 
and pH), along with increased galvanic corro-
sion. Furthermore, bacteria, algae and fungi can 
multiply. Water with excessive chloride will 
promote strong corrosion of stainless steel and 
non-ferrous metals, too. Increased corrosion can 
also be caused by ions of copper and other 
heavy metals.
• Depending on the used materials (e.g. 
aluminum or copper), deionized water with low 
conductance (and without corrosion inhibitors) 
can also encourage excessive corrosion. Deion-
ized water without corrosion inhibitors should 
ideally be used only with compatible materials 
such as stainless steel or nickel. It might be 
necessary to monitor and regulate the water 
coolant’s conductance via suitable construc-
tional measures.
• Adding a corrosion inhibitor will delay – but not 
prevent – galvanic corrosion. Corrosion inhibi-
tors bind with and neutralize ions in solution. In 
the process, the inhibitors are consumed and 
must be replenished regularly.

5.5
Power Supply
5.5.1Specifications
The varioSCAN II requires a balanced DC power 
source of 30 V (29 - 33 V) or 48 V (±2 V), or alterna-
tively a balanced DC power source of ±(15+1,5) V, 
with a maximum current of 1,5 A. The residual DC 
ripple should not exceed 100 mVPP.
The varioSCAN II has reverse polarity protection and 
inrush current limiting. The power supply and data 
interface are galvanically isolated from each other.
The power supply is monitored by the DSCB servo 
control board (see "Assuring Reliable Power" on 
page 16).
For information about the power supply require-
ments of your XY scan system, refer to its corre-
sponding manual.
5.5.2Connecting
The varioSCAN II’s DSCB servo control board must be 
connected to a SCANLAB digital interface board. The 
varioSCAN II will then receive its power from the 
DSCB board’s 3-pin POWER IN connector (see (4) in 
figure 11 on page 31).
When connecting the power supply, observe the 
following points:
• To ensure reliable operation, the varioSCAN II or 
its clamping block must be electrically insulated 
(see chapter 4.1 on page 22).
• The varioSCAN II or its digital interface board are 
separately connected to the power supply.
• The cable connecting the power source to the 
varioSCAN II must be shielded and should have a 
cross-sectional area of at least 1.5 mm2 per pole 
and a length not exceeding 6 m. RFI must be mini-
mized by connecting the cable’s shielding at one 
end (utilizing a large surface area) to the power 
supply’s metal shielding, and at the varioSCAN II’s 
end to the D-Sub connector’s housing.
• The varioSCAN II is usually operated with a 30 V 
power supply, but operation with two ±15 V 
power supplies is also possible (see figure 28). 
Another option is the 48 V power supply (see 
figure 29).
Though power supplies are typically not included 
in the scope of delivery, they are optionally avail-
able from SCANLAB.

6.1.1Digital Control via a SCANLAB

If the varioSCAN II’s DSCB digital servo control board

If the DSCB digital servo control board is connected

correction file, the RTC board:
• calculates appropriate varioSCAN II focal lengths

• compensates distortions of the image field

The correction file is specific for a certain working 
distance, field size and optical configuration of the 
varioSCAN II. For details, refer to the section "3D 
Correction Table Assignment" on page 52 or the RTC 
manual.
In the RTC command set, the desired X, Y and Z values 
for the XY scan system and the varioSCAN II must be 
specified as bit values, which are calculated via multi-
plying the desired values in [mm] by the calibration 
factor K in [bits/mm]. The calibration factor K in 
[bits/mm] and the usable scan volume (X x Y x Z in 
[mm]) are listed in the separate data sheet “Charac-
teristics of the 3-Axis Configuration”.
Figure 5 on page 18 shows the reference system for 
the image field which is used by the RTC boards. The 
Y axis points in the reverse direction of the entering 
laser beam, the Z axis points in the reverse direction 
of the emerging laser beam (X axis, Y axis and Z axis 
form a right-handed reference system). The extremal 
positions of the movable diverging optic are reached 
via the maximum and minimum allowed Z values (set 
values) +32767 and –32768.
An increase of the specified bit value moves the 
varioSCAN II’s diverging optic in the direction of the 
beam entrance, thus decreasing the varioSCAN II’s 
focal length.

XY2-100 Status Word
Five seconds after every reboot or reset, an XY2-100 
status word is transmitted. The status word contains 
three status values that can be queried via the RTC 
command get_head_status:
• PWROK (“Power OK”)
PWROK =0 signifies a problem in the power 
supply. In normal operation (after the electronic 
components have reached a stable operating 
state), the PWROK signal is 1. 
If the signal switches from 1 to 0 during opera-
tion, then the laser must be turned off immedi-
ately. Under some circumstances, the 
varioSCAN II could focus the laser beam to an 
unintended position, which may cause serious 
health hazards and severe equipment damage. 
The system should be checked immediately to 
determine the cause. PWROK=0 can be caused, 
for example, by a defective power supply.
• TEMPOK (“Temperature OK”)
The TEMPOK signal switches from 0 to 1 within a 
few seconds after power-up.
• POSACK (“Position Acknowledge”)
POSACK = 1 signifies that the difference between 
the set value and the real position is less than 
0.56% of the movable diverging optic’s maximum 
travel range. The POSACK signal normally 
switches to 1 within a few seconds after power-
up. The command control_command allows you 
to change the POSACK threshold value (see 
"Process Monitoring" on page 56).
The Z return channel returns the varioSCAN II’s three 
XY2-100 status values separately from any status 
values of the XY scan system (in contrast, the X and 
Y return channels of a 2-axis or 3-axis scan system 
both return a logically ANDed POSACK signal of the 
X and Y axis).

6.5
Tracking Error Compensation
The varioSCAN II’s tracking error typically lags that of 
the XY scan system. This can, for example, produce 
unfavorable laser processing results due to focus lag 
when processing inclined surfaces.
If control of the focusing system and XY scan system 
is performed by a SCANLAB RTC6 board, then 
tracking error compensation can be activated to 
delay signals to the XY scan system. For this, use the 
RTC6 command set_timelag_compensation (see 
the RTC6 manual).
The varioSCAN II’s tracking error is listed in Part 2 of 
this manual, and the scan head’s tracking error is 
specified in the scan head’s manual.
Even if the racking error compensation is applied, the 
XY scan system and the varioSCAN II show a different 
behavior during the acceleration phase. As a result, 
incorrect focusing can still occur during acceleration. 
Only after the final speed has been reached is the 
dynamic defocusing also constant or disappears. The 
acceleration time required here corresponds to twice 
the larger tracking error (that of the XY scan system 
or that of the Z axis). Even when using the tracking 
error compensation, the delays must be selected 
accordingly for minimum dynamic defocusing.
6.5.1Using the varioSCAN II with 
excelliSCAN and SCANahead Control
If the varioSCAN II is used in a 3D configuration with 
an excelliSCAN, SCANLAB recommends using the 
tracking error compensation of the RTC6 board 
(set_timelag_compensation). Since the excelliSCAN 
with the SCANahead control does not have a tracking 
error, the PreviewTime is used here as a comparison 
value to the tracking error of the varioSCAN II (further 
details on this can be found in the manuals of the 
RTC6 and the excelliSCAN). Thus, after reaching the 
final speed, the focusing error disappears. During the 
acceleration phase, however, faulty focusing may 
occur again. If the occurrence is to be suppressed, the 
AccScale value can be set to 0. This switches the laser 
off during the acceleration phase.
As a rule, the maximum positioning and marking 
speed of the 3D configuration consisting of 
varioSCAN II and excelliSCAN is limited by the 
maximum running speed of the varioSCAN II.
6.5.2High Dynamic Requirements
In general, the maximum achievable speeds for the 
XY scan system and the Z axis are different for a 
3D configuration. The maximum speed of the 
3D configuration is limited by the slower of the two 
speeds. When controlling with a strong dynamic 
load, the maximum speed of the 3D configuration 
must therefore be taken into account. If this is 
exceeded, focusing errors can occur constantly over 
the entire travel range and the varioSCAN II can reach 
the mechanical edge stops for the lens positions.

7.5
Alignment
To ensure optimum performance of the laser scan-
ning system, it is crucial that the varioSCAN II, the 
laser beam and the XY scan system (scan head or scan 
module) are precisely aligned with respect to each 
other. Incorrect alignment might lead to the 
following effects:
• vignetting of the laser beam
• impaired focus quality
• arbitrary translation of the image field
In most cases, the first-mentioned effect will be 
present. Vignetting occurs if the laser beam is not 
able to fully pass through the optical components. 
Part of the beam cross section will be cut off, 
reducing the working beam’s power. Additionally, 
scan system damage may readily occur due to exces-
sive absorption of laser power. Therefore, the align-
ment procedure must be carried out with 
considerable care.
7.5.1Recommended Tolerances
To avoid vignetting, the system must be aligned as 
precisely as possible. If the laser beam profile is 
gaussian, the maximum tolerances appropriate for 
most applications are:
Beam Position
(relative to the varioSCAN II)’s optical axis 
Relative Orientation of XY Scan System and 
varioSCAN II
(relative to the input aperture of the XY scan system)
If you use a laser with a flat top beam profile, it can 
be necessary to align the scan system with even 
higher precision. Avoid vignetting, especially with 
very high power lasers. Align as carefully as possible 
and – if necessary – reduce the beam diameter to an 
appropriate value.
7.5.2Aligning the Components
Prior to aligning and adjusting the components, you 
must carry out the following steps:
Suitably arrange all components of the system, 
including the laser, the beam expander (optional), 
adjustable mirrors and the XY scan system (see 
figure 30 on page 60).
If present, remove protective caps from the 
varioSCAN II’s beam entrance and beam exit.
For alignment, follow the steps listed below:
The first element to align is the beam expander. 
Adjust its position so that the laser beam

7.5.4Adjusting the Working Distance
To ensure that the scan system delivers good results, 
the following three important parameters must 
match:
• The working distance between the XY scan 
system and the z=0 working plane (see 
distance A in figure 30 on page 60).
(The working distance is defined as the distance 
between the entering beam’s optical axis and the 
nominal working plane.
If the XY scan system is used with an F-Theta 
objective, the working distance at z=0 corre-
sponds to the nominal working plane of the 
objective)
• The focus position or back focal length
• The assigned correction table’s coefficient A, 
used for control via an RTC board
If there are large deviations from the specified values, 
the size of the image field will change and the correc-
tion file will not correctly compensate field distor-
tions. Thus, the working distance of the scan system 
must be accurately adjusted before the scan system 
can be placed into operation. (The two remaining 
parameters do not need to be adjusted in this proce-
dure.)

distance depends on the components used. If you’re

as follows:
SCANLAB provides you with a correction table 
matched to your scan system and your requirements. 
The working distance is a key input variable for calcu-
lating this correction table. You can find the value 
used for the working distance in both the correction 
table’s ReadMe.txt file and the “Characteristics of the 3-
Axis Configuration” data sheet.
Adjust the working distance and focus position as 
follows:
First adjust the working distance (see A in

Turn on the controlling PC containing the RTC

Turn on the power supply for the varioSCAN II 
and the XY scan system.
Move the laser focus to the reference point

Scan a test pattern onto the working plane as

Based on the marking results, adjust the 
varioSCAN II’s objective for an optimal focus 
position by manually turning the focusing ring.

7.5.5Checking the Z-Axis Calibration
When the laser focus is moved to a desired point 
within the image field, the RTC board adjusts the 
focal length of the varioSCAN II accordingly. Here, 
the RTC board uses a correction table included in the 
3D correction file supplied with the RTC software (for 
details, refer to the RTC manual or the "RTC 3D Soft-
ware" manual).
The exact output values for the varioSCAN II depend 
on various parameters such as beam divergence and 
the tolerances of the optical components. Therefore, 
in some cases the pre-calculated correction table 
might not fit optimally to the individual scan system.
To check if this is the case, SCANLAB recommends the 
following procedure:
Run a laser marking test that covers the entire 
image field and, if necessary, the maximum focus 
shift in the Z direction. Check if the laser focus 
meets your application’s requirements.
If you find significant levels of incorrect spot 
positioning, it might be necessary to re-calibrate 
the Z-axis correction table.
7.6
Monitoring Operating 
Temperature and Current 
Draw
The varioSCAN II can be optionally equipped with a 
water-cooled beam entrance aperture at the beam 
entrance to dissipate heat generated by absorbed 
laser power.
In a test, determine whether operation with a water-
cooled entrance aperture is required for your 
varioSCAN II in the given application (see chapter 4.2 
on page 25). 
If excessive laser power or laser power density is 
applied at the varioSCAN II’s entrance, then the 
varioSCAN II’s optical components or motor can be 
destroyed. Therefore, follow the specifications for 
maximum laser power and the specifications for 
operating conditions (see Part 2 of this manual).
Operating the varioSCAN II with excessive current 
draw will heat up the electronics. Therefore, the 
current consumption of the DSCB servo control board 
should not exceed the value of 0.85 A RMS (26 W). If 
current draw passes a certain threshold, the 
varioSCAN II’s output stage will very quickly switch 
off. Such exceedances are particularly caused by 
incorrect delays or excessive dynamic loads. However, 
the error is actually indicated as temperature critical 
(see RTC5 or RTC6 manual, control_command, Stop 
Event Code = 00030H).
In this case, do not wait for the varioSCAN II to cool 
down. Instead shut down the system and then restart 
it. For further processing, you’ll have to reduce 
current draw (e.g. by checking delays and reducing 
speeds).
Here, also observe the warning on current draw and 
temperature in chapter 4.2 on page 25)

9.1
Fault Diagnosis and 
Functional Test
The varioSCAN II’s versatile status return functions 
can also be used for system diagnostics. Information 
such as the following can be queried:
• the current operating state
• the operating state at the moment of the most 
recently occurred operation interruption
• an event code indicating which particular event 
caused the change to an error state
(Also see "Safety During Installation and Operation" 
on page 9).
In general, all status information can even be read 
after the varioSCAN II has entered an error state due 
to an internal protective mechanism (provided that 
the varioSCAN II is still sufficiently powered).
Data transfer between the RTC board and the 
varioSCAN II can be tested by transmitting an 8-bit 
value to the varioSCAN II via the command 
control_command. Subsequently, a 16-bit value will 
be returned on the corresponding status channel: If 
data transfer is error-free, then the upper 8 bits of the 
returned 16-bit value will be identical with the origi-
nally sent 8-bit value, and the lower 8 bits will be 
identical with the complement of the sent 8-bit value. 
These 16-bit values will be returned until the 
control_command is used to select another return 
data type.
The command control_command allows the 
currently used retransmission data type to be tempo-
rarily stored for later retrieval. This lets you restore 
whatever original return behavior was in effect 
before you conducted the data transfer test.
