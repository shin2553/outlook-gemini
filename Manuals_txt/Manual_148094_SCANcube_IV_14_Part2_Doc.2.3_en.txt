Electrical Connections
The scan head provides a 25-pin female D-SUB 
connector for power supply and digital data transfer, 
which follows the XY2-100 protocoll.
Configure a suitable cable for the connection of the 
power supply and the data transfer. A suitable Y cable 
is available from SCANLAB, see chapter 4.3 "Y Cable 
(optional)" on page 12.
4.1
Power Supply
The scan head requires a power supply of 30 V
(29 - 33 V) and a maximum current of 3 A. The resi-
dual ripple of the power source should not exceed 
100 mVSS.
Only use a power supply with soft start.
The scan head is powered via a 30 V power supply 
(see figure 4). In general, the power supply is not 
included in the package, but suitable power supplies 
are available from SCANLAB. SCANLAB recommends 
to connect the 0 V connection of the power supply’s 
output side (or the scan head’s GND connection) to 
the mains's grounding wire (PE).
There should be no more than ±5 V potential diffe-
rence between the ground of the scan head’s power 
source and the ground of the control computer. 
Usually the ground of the control computer is 
connected to the mains’s grounding wire (PE) via the 
computer’s mains cable. Therefore, excessive poten-
tial differences between the two grounds can be 
generally avoided by connecting the power supply’s 
0 V connection to the mains's grounding wire (PE) via 
a short as possible cable and as close as possible to 
the PC ground.

The scan head provides a 25-pin female D-SUB 
connector for data transfer and power supply (30 V).

4.1.2Connection
Connect the power supply to the respective pins of 
the 25-pin female D-SUB connector (+30 V / GND, 
see figure 5) as follows:
Make sure each power connection has the correct 
polarity.
Connect each pole of the power source via an 
appropriate cable to all pins of the scan head’s 
connector. The cable connecting the power 
supply and the scan head must be shielded and 
should have a cross-sectional area of at least 
1.5 mm2 per pole and a length not exceeding 6 
meters. RFI must be minimized by connecting the 
cable’s shielding at one end (utilizing a large 
surface area) to the power supply’s metal 
shielding and at the scanhead-end to the 
housing’s SUB-D connector.
– connect the power source GND to all three GND 
pins of the 25-pin female D-SUB connector
– connect the +30 V power source to all three 
+30 V pins of the 25-pin female D-SUB 
connector
Caution!
• Before proceeding with wiring the scan head, 
make sure none of the wires carry any voltages. 
The control electronics and the computer must 
be turned off.
• Turn off the power supply before disconnecting 
the power or the data cable.
• Follow all power supply electrical specifications 
exactly.

4.2
Data Transfer
The signals between scan head and controller are 
transmmitted by a data cable (when controlling with 
an RTC5 board, an XY2-100 converter is additionally 
necessary). For a description of the transmitted 
signals, see chapter 5 "Controlling the Scan System" 
on page 13.
4.2.1Data Cable Guidlines
Data cables are generally not included in the 
package. SCANLAB recommends the following 
design (for electrical data transmission):
• The cable should not be longer than 10 m. If a 
longer data cable is needed, the synchronization 
of the (bidirectional) communication between 
scan head and RTC control board should be 
adjusted. If you control the scan head via an RTC3, 
RTC4 or RTC SCANalone board, this adjustment 
can be realized via the RTC command 
set_piso_control (see RTC manual; hereby the 
cable length must not exceed 20 m). If you 
control the scan head via an RTC5 board, the 
adjustment can be realized via configuring the 
XY2-100 converter’s solder jumpers (see RTC5 
manual; hereby cable lengths above 25 m are not 
recommended).
• The data cable should have 25-pin male SUB-D 
connectors with identical pin-out at both ends. 
The connection channels (SYNC±, CLOCK±, 
CHAN1±, CHAN2±, STATUS±, STATUS1±) should 
be realized as a twisted cable pair, each.
• The data cable must have coaxial copper braided 
shielding.
• The D-SUB connectors must have fully shielded 
metal housings.
• The electrical connection of the cable’s braided 
shielding to the D-SUB housing should not be 
implemented as a wire. Instead, the cable’s 
braided shielding should be coaxially connected 
to the D-SUB housing via shielded clamps.
• The data cable’s controller end must be fitted 
with a ferrit ring (e.g. Würth WE 742 711 32).
Figure 6 on page 11 shows the data cable layout and 
pin assignments.
The scan head uses a single connector to provide 
both the power supply voltages and the data signals. 
Therefore SCANLAB recommends implementing a 
cabling solution that allows the use of separate 
cables for data and power. The data section of such 
a cabling solution should be designed to accommo-
date the data cable shown in figure 6.

5
Controlling the Scan System
5.1
Data Transmission
The positions of the galvanometer scanners are 
controlled via the transmission of digital input 
signals. The controller and the scan system are inter-
connected via a serial interface. Data transmission 
follows the XY2-100 protocol. In the process, essen-
tially the following signals are transferred:
• The controller delivers set values for the scanner 
positions.
• The scan system generates status signals to be 
returned to the controller.
• Two additional channels transmit
the data transport synchronization signal
and a clock signal.
Figure 8 shows the timing of the clock signal 
(CLOCK), the synchronization signal (SYNC) and the 
three data channels (CHAN1/CHAN2/CHAN3).
Every 10 µs, three 20-bit words (3 control bits, 16 
data bits, 1 parity bit) are transmitted serially as 
differential signals.
5.2
Position Signals, Image Field 
and X-Y Reference System
Position signals are digitally transferred from the 
controller to the scan head. 
Figure 9 shows the definition of the X-Y reference 
system which is used for the position signals trans-
mitted to the scan head. The orientation of the axes 
corresponds to the orientation used by the RTC 
boards from SCANLAB: The Y axis points in the oppo-
site direction of the entry beam (and the Z axis in the 
opposite direction of the exit beam). Consequently: 
Scanner 1 deflects the beam in the Y direction, 
Scanner 2 in the X direction.
The scan head is calibrated in such a way that for a 
scan angle of 0.408 rad optically with excursion in 
the negative axis direction the bit-value "1311" has to 
be transmitted, for the neutral position (null point) 
the bit-value "32768, and for a scan angle of 0.384 
rad optically with excursion in the positive axis direc-
tion the bit-value "64225".
The maximum adjustable scan angle is (1 / 0.96) 
larger than the calibration angle. The input signal 
values for the maximum adjustable image field points 
(see figure 9) are listed in the following table.
Vignetting can occur at a particular scan angle 
dependent on the specific scan head and objective. 
The laser beam is then partially blocked within the 
scan head or objective, which results in transmission 
losses. The higher the power loss, the greater is the 
risk of damage to the scan system. In view of this, the 
technical specifications on page 18 include not only 
the calibration angle, but also the maximum allowed 
scan angle. This is not the same as the maximum 
adjustable scan angle. To avoid scan system damage, 
make sure the maximum allowed scan angle is never 
exceeded.

Figure 9 also depicts the pillow-barrel-shaped distor-

field distortion is caused by the beam path within the 
scan head and by the characteristics of the objective. 
It must be compensated by the controller. 
If you use a SCANLAB RTC control board for 
controlling the scan head, the field distortion is 
compensated automatically. Before data values are 
transferred to the scan head, the values are trans-

between the input laser beam and the nominal 
working plane, depend on various factors – among 
them the focal length of the objective and the aper-
ture of the scan head. The divergence of the input 
beam also has an influence on the working

5.3
Monitoring of the Scan 
System with Integrated 
Sensors (optional)
This scan system is equipped with a temperature 
sensor on the servo boards PCB and temperature 
sensors for the galvanometer scanner 1 and 2. The 
scan system therefore offers advanced monitoring 
capabilities based on the evaluation of the sensor 
signal.
Default thresholds for the error status and for the 
warning signal have been configured for each sensor. 
During monitoring, the scan system returns an error 
status and/or warning message if the threshold 
values are violated.
Error Status
The error status is linked to the PowerOK bit. If the 
error status is "not ok", the PowerOK bit is 0. With 
set_laser_control() you can configure the signal 
suppression so that the laser switches off in this case, 
see RTC manual.
There are three possible causes for PowerOK = 0:
• The output stage of the galvo control is off 
(StopEvent ! = 0).
• One or more sensor signals have violated their 
error thresholds.
• The communication with the scan system is faulty 
(e. g. cable is not connected).
All monitored signals are combined to a linked error 
status. If one of the linked signals is "not ok", the error 
status is "not ok". Conversely, all linked signals must 
be "ok" so that the error status is also "ok".
For each available sensor signal, there is an upper 
threshold, above which an error is triggered (see 
table below).
Warning Signal
The warning signal is linked to the TempOK bit. The 
TempOK bit can be used for the automatic laser signal 
suppression.
There are three possible causes for TempOK = 0:
• Temperature warning: Temperature is outside the 
operating temperature.
• Temperature critical: galvo control switches off 
(PowerOK = 0).
• One or more sensor signals have violated their 
warning threshold.
All monitored signals are combined to a linked 
warning signal. If one of the linked signals is “not 
ok”, the warning signal is “not ok”. Conversely, all 
linked signals must be “ok” so that the warnig signal 
is also “ok”.
For each available sensor signal, there is an upper 
threshold, above which a warning message is trig-
gered (see table below).

5.4
Status Signals
The scan head provides a PWROK status signal (i.e. 
"Power OK") available via the XY2-100 protocol. If you 
use an RTC control board, then this status signal can 
be evaluated via the get_head_status or get_value 
commands.
The PWROK signal indicates the status of the power 
supply. Upon power-up, the PWROK signal is initially 
0. After less than one second (when the electronic 
components have reached a stable operating state) 
the PWROK signal then switches to 1. 
PWROK = 0 signifies a problem in the power supply 
or a protective action by the electronics. If, upon 
powering up, the PWROK signal doesn’t switch to 1 
within a second or if the signal switches from 1 to 0 
during operation, then the laser must be turned off 
immediately. Under some circumstances the system 
could deflect the laser beam in an unintended direc-
tion, which may cause health hazards and severe 
equipment damage. The system should be checked 
immediately to determine the cause. 
Switching of the PWROK status signal from 1 to 0 
during operation can be caused, for example, by a 
defective power supply (also see chapter 4.1 "Power 
Supply" on page 8).
5.5
Pre-processing tracking error 
control
The digital control consists of two components:
• Pre-processing
In comparison to conventional tracking error 
control, the pre-processing calculates a target 
trajectory with tracking error from the RTC control 
values. The duration of the pre-processing is 
referred to as ControlPreview. The ControlPreview 
is indicated in the technical specifications (see 
chapter 6 "SCANcube IV 14 - Technical Specifica-
tions" on page 18).
• Control
The galvanometer scanner control follows this 
target trajectory with a small trajectory error 
(actual trajectory).
This type of control has the following advantages:
• Precise tracking errors with significantly reduced 
specimen scatter (<1%)
• Optional read-back of the status signals PosAck or 
TrAck
For synchronous control of the scan system and laser, 
the tracking error and the ControlPreview must be 
taken into account in the control signals for the laser. 
This also applies to all other control signals that are 
to be executed synchronously with the movement of 
the scan system.
5.5.1Adjustment of the control for 
systems with pre-processing tracking 
error control
General
The actual position is additionally delayed compared 
to the control values. This means that the times of the 
laser control signals must also be shifted in relation 
to the target position specification.
• Shift of the laser control signals = ControlPreview 
+ tracking error
When using a RTC control card, this has no effect on 
the duration of a job. The switching times of the laser 
are executed after the position calculation of the job 
in a buffer memory of the RTC control card. The next 
job can already be executed.
SCANLAB recommends shifting the switching times 
of the laser so that the desired proportion of accele-
ration and deceleration phase is also achieved:
• Shift the switch-on points of the laser forward by 
approx. 40% to fade in the acceleration phase.
Switch-on delay of the laser (laser-on-delay) = 
60% * tracking error + ControlPreview
• Shift the switch-off points of the laser back by 
approx. 20% to fade in the deceleration phase.
Switch-off delay of the laser (laser-off delay) = 
120% * tracking error + ControlPreview.
The ControlPreview has no influence on the scanner 
delays.

This compensation is activated via the

Handling with tracking error
The handling of the tracking error is essentially iden-
tical to the systems with conventional tracking error 
control.
These empirical values apply to the scanner delays:
• Jump-Delay: 200% of the tracking error
• Mark-Delay: 100% of the tracking error
• Polygon-Delay: 50% of the tracking error
When comparing the actual position with the target 
position, ControlPreview and tracking error must also 
be taken into account.
In systems with conventional tracking error control, 
the tracking error is a characteristic of the scanning 
system. Depending on the tuning, the tracking error 
can vary between different examples of the same 
scanning system.
For systems with ControlPreview, the tracking error is 
a characteristic of the pre-processing. The tracking 
error is identical for all examples of the same scan-
ning system.
